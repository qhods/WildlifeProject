Bio

The Galapagos Giant Tortoise lives up to its name weighing about 475 pounds and around 4 feet tall. They also live in the wild for 100 or more years, one of the oldest recorded tortoises age was 152. They are considered to be vulnerable which means they are at high risk of extinction. These animals live a simple lifestyle that consists of eating, relaxing, and sleeping. The Giant Tortoise naps for about 16 hours a day and the other hours they lay in the sun or eating grass, leaves, fruit, and cactus. Eating is not on the top of their list like other animals. This is because they have such a slow metabolism and can store water, it allows them to survive without consuming food or water for up to a year. If it is colder out you can find them trying to warm up in the mud, water, or some type of brush. Mating can happen all year but it is at its highest between January and August. After, the female tortoise will find dry and sandy ground to dig a nest where the eggs will be laid. From there, 120 to 140 days after the female laid the eggs, the babies leave their eggs trying to leave the nest which can take up to a month of digging up to the surface.

Population Decrease

There are only about 15,000 Giant Tortoises left which is a decrease from around 250,000 of them. Darwin, known for evolution, found 15 different species of Giant Tortoises at the time. Now there are only about 10 different species left. This is because tortoises were hunted by pirates, whalers, and merchantmen from the 17th century all the way until the 19th century. This was one way these animals have become endangered. Another reason is because of animals such as:

  1. pigs
  2. dogs
  3. cats
  4. rats
  5. goats
  6. cattle
These animals are not native to the island which is causing competition for food as well as endangering the eggs of the tortoise.

What can be done?

Most of the Giant Tortoise species have been declared endangered or critically endangered. The Ecuadorian government has been protecting the different tortoise species since 1970. The Charles Darwin Research Station has been captive breeding which has been an effective measure to increase the population. The Galapagos Conservancy has been working on reducing the amount of nonnative animals that are on the islands. This allows for the eggs to not be disturbed and more grazing area for the tortoises. People can look up organized trips to the galapagos where you would volunteer for a week or so and help the tortoises.
